AP PHYSICS 1 The Physics of Running

Newton's Laws in Motion: Every footstrike creates a ground reaction force (≈2–3× body weight). By Newton's 3rd Law, the ground pushes back equally — this propels you forward. Your running efficiency depends on minimizing braking force and maximizing forward impulse (J = F·Δt).
Work & Energy: W = F·d. Running on hills increases work done against gravity (W = mgh). Flat running work ≈ overcoming air resistance + internal friction. Power output P = W/t tells you how "strong" your engine is — elite marathoners sustain ~300W.
Momentum & Impulse: p = mv. A heavier or faster runner has more momentum. Each stride cycle involves decelerating (losing momentum at contact) then accelerating (gaining it at push-off). Minimizing that loss = running economy.

AP CHEM Cellular Respiration Chemistry

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ~38 ATP (ΔG = −2870 kJ/mol)
Glycolysis (cytoplasm): Glucose → 2 pyruvate + 2 ATP + 2 NADH. This happens whether or not O₂ is present. At easy paces this feeds the aerobic pathway. At hard efforts, pyruvate → lactate (lactic acid) to regenerate NAD⁺ and keep glycolysis running — this is the "burn" you feel.
Krebs Cycle (mitochondrial matrix): Each acetyl-CoA yields 2CO₂, 3NADH, 1FADH₂, 1 ATP. Your CO₂ output directly reflects this cycle's activity — your respiratory rate is literally a readout of the Krebs cycle speed.
Oxidative Phosphorylation (inner mitochondrial membrane): NADH/FADH₂ donate electrons to the electron transport chain → H⁺ gradient → ATP synthase produces ~34 ATP. O₂ is the final electron acceptor. Without O₂, the whole chain halts — hence the importance of breathing.
Buffer Chemistry: CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. During hard runs, CO₂ rises → blood pH drops → you breathe harder. This is your body's bicarbonate buffer system (Henderson–Hasselbalch equation) in real time.

AP BIO Physiology & Adaptation

Muscle Fiber Types: Type I (slow-twitch) — high mitochondria density, fatigue-resistant, aerobic. Type IIa (fast-twitch oxidative) — mixed. Type IIx (fast-twitch glycolytic) — powerful, fatigue quickly. Easy runs train Type I; sprint intervals recruit Type II. Endurance training causes Type IIx → IIa conversion.
Cardiovascular Adaptation: Cardiac output = HR × Stroke Volume (Q = HR × SV). Training increases SV (heart gets bigger, pumps more per beat) — your resting HR drops. Elite runners have resting HR ~40 bpm vs ~70 average. Each beat delivers more O₂ to working muscles.
VO₂ Max: Maximum rate of O₂ consumption (mL O₂/kg/min). Determined by cardiac output × arteriovenous O₂ difference (Fick principle). Untrained: ~35–45. Trained: 50–70. Elite marathoners: 70–85+. Training increases mitochondrial density (more ETC capacity) and capillary density.
Hormonal Response: Epinephrine (adrenaline) → ↑HR, ↑glycogen breakdown. Cortisol → mobilizes fats. Testosterone/GH → muscle repair post-run. Endorphins → bind opioid receptors → "runner's high." All mediated through signal transduction cascades you'll master in AP Bio.
